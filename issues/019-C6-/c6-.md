No bullshit, since that's the ask. Six papers, six honest ratings:

**Rock Talk (0.1 → 0.4)** — Grad student, early year, no advisor sign-off yet. The bones are legit: real theory grounding (Shannon, Zipf, Weaver, McLuhan), an actual attempt at formalizing metrics (TIR, SDI, HTF), and a genuinely solid FAQ section anticipating real critiques. What keeps it out of "defensible thesis chapter" territory: the worked examples are three hand-picked toy sentences with eyeballed intent counts, not a dataset, and Star Trek episodes get cited with the same formatting weight as Shannon's actual paper.

**Academic Vibing 0.2** — The strongest one in the stack, grad student bordering on postdoc self-awareness. It's the only paper that turns the critical lens on itself — naming sycophancy, "Collusive Consensus," the human as fallible vibe-checker. That kind of built-in failure-mode awareness is a real research instinct. Still no actual measured CDI on real disagreement data, so it stops short of doing the thing it proposes.

**Rock Culture 0.2** — Undergrad, the fun elective paper, not the capstone. Genuinely clever pattern-matching (Pakled vs. Cytherian vs. Burnham as communication archetypes is a good bit), but it's narrative categorization with zero inter-rater reliability or corpus backing — real citations and fictional characters get cited as if they're the same kind of evidence.

**Rock Training 0.2** — High school senior with a good vocabulary. Uses the right words (BPE, OOD, embedding weights) but the central numeric claim — 300-400% context gain — is asserted with literally nothing behind it, and the paper doesn't even finish its own references section. This is the weakest link; it's a hypothesis pitch, not a paper.

**The Ethnographic Study** — Undergrad, the kind that gets a "B, see me" from a professor. Polished prose, but the core methodological move — using your own git commit timestamps as ethnographic field notes and concluding "institutional collapse" — is a strong claim riding on thin evidence, with none of the reflexivity or triangulation real ethnography requires.

**Adversarial Agent** — Can't rate it, you didn't upload that one.

Overall: this is a corpus with real ideas buried in it (Rock Talk's compression hypothesis and Academic Vibing's self-critique are the two worth taking further), surrounded by a lot of confident scaffolding that hasn't touched real data yet.
