Let me actually break this down rather than throw out a single number, because the components have wildly different timescales in the traditional world.

What we actually did tonight, mapped to the academic pipeline:
- Literature review across 4 distinct domains (interruption/attention research, extended mind/distributed cognition, alcohol/THC/nicotine cognitive effects, information theory) — a competent grad student does a real lit review like this in 2–4 weeks per domain if they're already in the field, longer if it's adjacent to their specialty. You don't casually pull Sweller, Hutchins, Clark & Chalmers, Mark et al., AND a cannabis-cognition meta-analysis in one sitting unless you already had them half-memorized. Call it 6–10 weeks, realistically, for someone competent but not already expert in all four.
- Drafting a full paper with abstract, methodology, citations, and an honest limitations section — for someone who already has the lit review done, a first full draft is 2–4 weeks, then multiple revision passes based on their advisor's notes, typically another month or two of back-and-forth before it's advisor-approved.
- The actual hard part — catching your own bullshit (v0.9's leakage, the STFU Attractor, mythology bleeding into methodology) — this is normally where a committee member or peer reviewer earns their keep, and it happens after submission, as external critique you then have to react to. That's typically 2–6 months of review-cycle latency in a real PhD program, sometimes longer depending on the venue. Tonight, that critique happened in minutes, conversationally, and you fixed it in the same sitting instead of waiting months to find out.
- Building actual tooling (the triage skill, the compression matrix design) — this is normally a side project a grad student does between paper deadlines, if at all. Call it a few weeks of stolen time, generously.

Rough stack-up, being honest rather than hyperbolic: somewhere around 4–7 months of real calendar time for a competent PhD student working at a normal, sustainable pace — most of that being literature review depth and review-cycle latency, not raw writing time. Compressed here into one evening, mostly because the review cycle that normally takes months happened in real-time conversation instead of through the mail.

The honest caveat, which the methodology itself would insist I add: this isn't "a year of PhD work," it's specifically the early-stage drafting-and-stress-testing phase. The actual dissertation-grade rigor — committee defense, replication, the slow accountability structures — still doesn't exist yet and isn't something tonight produced. What got compressed was the part of the process this method was always honest about targeting: getting to a defensible draft fast, not getting to peer-reviewed truth fast. Worth keeping that distinction sharp, especially in something you might paste into an issue at 2am feeling good about the number.
