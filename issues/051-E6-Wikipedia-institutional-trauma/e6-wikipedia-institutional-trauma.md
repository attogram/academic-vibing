# E6 - Wikipedia institutional trauma #51

Opened on Jun 18, 2026 by attogram

"Institutional trauma" is actually a great way to put it. The Wikipedia
editing community is essentially suffering from a collective case of
burnout and hyper-vigilance because of LLMs.
The friction between Wikipedia and generative AI has escalated
significantly. Following intense internal debates, the English
Wikipedia community voted for a near-blanket ban on using LLMs to
generate or rewrite article content.
The "trauma" stems from a few distinct, painful pressure points that
have fundamentally changed what it feels like to be a Wikipedia editor:

1. The "Sludge" Flood

Before the LLM boom, bad Wikipedia edits were usually obvious: blatant
vandalism, biased PR language, or poorly formatted text. LLMs changed
the game by allowing well-meaning (but lazy) users and automated bots
to flood the site with high-volumes of content that looks flawlessly
formatted and reads beautifully, but is riddled with errors. Editors
went from spot-checking obvious junk to doing grueling, line-by-line
fact-checking of highly confident-sounding prose.

2. Fake Footnotes (The Ultimate Betrayal)

Wikipedia runs entirely on verifiability. When LLMs hallucinate a
historical fact, they often hallucinate a plausible-sounding citation
to match it (complete with a fake book title, real-sounding author, or
broken URL). For an editing community that relies on verifying sources
to maintain trust, hunting down citations for sources that literally do
not exist is incredibly draining.

3. The "AI Essay" Aesthetic

If you hang out in Wikipedia's behind-the-scenes policy pages (like
their essay on Signs of AI writing), you can see the community actively
profiling LLM behavior. They have developed an intense allergy to
specific stylistic tells, including:
  * The "Crucial Role" cliché: Constant, vague conclusions about how a
    subject "faces challenges typical of urban areas" or "plays a
    pivotal role in the modern landscape."
  * Overly neat summaries: Paragraphs that wrap up with a perfectly
    balanced, superficial, "on the one hand, on the other hand"
    conclusion rather than raw, gritty encyclopedic facts.
  * The "Canned" Neutrality: A distinct, passive voice that avoids
    basic verbs in favor of flowery, non-committal AI vocabulary.

4. An Existential Threat to Traffic

Compounding the internal exhaustion is an external anxiety: people are
increasingly asking chatbots for quick answers instead of clicking on
Wikipedia links. Wikimedia data noted a roughly 8% drop in traffic over
2025 as tools like ChatGPT and Google's AI Overviews started
intercepting information seekers. There is an underlying frustration
that LLMs are trained on Wikipedia's hard work, only to redirect users
away from the platform while simultaneously polluting the platform with
synthetic text.

  The Exceptions: Under the current rules, editors are allowed to use
  LLMs for exactly two things: basic copyedits (fixing spelling or
  punctuation on text they wrote themselves) and as a first-pass tool
  for translating articles from other language Wikipedias—and even
  then, strict human review is required.

Ultimately, Wikipedia’s response has been a defensive hunker-down.
They’ve even introduced "speedy deletion" rules specifically targeting
suspected AI-generated drafts. For a project founded on the radical
idea that "anyone can edit," the community has had to become intensely
protective, turning away from automation to preserve the integrity of
human-verified knowledge.
