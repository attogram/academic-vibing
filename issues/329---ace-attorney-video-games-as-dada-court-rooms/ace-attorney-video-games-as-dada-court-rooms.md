Abstract

This paper examines Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney as a form of dadaist courtroom performance, analyzing how the franchise constructs a surreal legal universe that simultaneously critiques, exaggerates, and reimagines the adversarial trial system. Using the BuzzFeed Multiplayer video “Real Lawyer Goes to Court in Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney” (2019) as a contemporary cultural artifact, the paper situates the game within discourses of legal realism, procedural rhetoric, ludic epistemology, and media performativity. The analysis argues that Ace Attorney’s courtroom is not merely unrealistic—it is intentionally anti‑realist, functioning as a playful yet incisive commentary on the spectacle of justice, the instability of truth, and the theatricality inherent in legal practice.

---

1. Introduction: When Law Becomes Theater

The BuzzFeed video featuring criminal defense attorney Diana Aizman reacting to Ace Attorney provides a unique entry point into the tension between real legal procedure and video‑game legal fantasy. Her reactions—oscillating between amusement, disbelief, and professional critique—highlight the gap between:

- Law as practiced (structured, rule‑bound, procedural)
- Law as imagined (dramatic, chaotic, personality‑driven)

This gap is precisely where Ace Attorney thrives.

The franchise transforms the courtroom into a stage, the trial into a battle, and legal reasoning into a puzzle‑solving performance. The result is a legal world that resembles dada art: absurd, self‑aware, and deliberately destabilizing conventional expectations of rationality.

---

2. Theoretical Framework

2.1 Dadaism and the Logic of Absurdity

Dadaism emerged in the early 20th century as a reaction against the rationalism that artists believed had led to global catastrophe. Its core principles include:

- Absurdity as critique
- Subversion of institutional authority
- Collage, fragmentation, and non‑linear meaning
- Performance as disruption

Ace Attorney’s courtroom embodies these principles. The judge is forgetful, witnesses are unhinged, and evidence appears with theatrical flourish. Truth is not discovered but performed.

2.2 Legal Realism and the Myth of Objectivity

Legal realism argues that law is shaped by:

- Human biases
- Institutional pressures
- Narrative framing
- Social context

Ace Attorney exaggerates these elements to reveal their presence in real legal systems. The BuzzFeed lawyer’s commentary underscores this: she repeatedly notes that while the game is unrealistic, it exposes real anxieties about evidence, advocacy, and judicial discretion.

2.3 Ludic Epistemology: How Games Produce Knowledge

Games teach players how to “know” within their worlds. Ace Attorney’s epistemology is:

- Evidence‑driven
- Contradiction‑focused
- Narratively structured
- Player‑centered

Truth emerges not from procedure but from player insight, reinforcing the idea that legal truth is constructed rather than discovered.

---

3. The Ace Attorney Courtroom as Dada Space

3.1 The Judge as Absurd Authority

The judge in Ace Attorney is:

- Forgetful
- Easily manipulated
- Emotionally reactive
- Comically incompetent

This is not accidental. It is a dadaist critique of judicial authority, revealing the fragility of the figure who is supposed to embody impartiality.

3.2 Witnesses as Performers

Witnesses in Ace Attorney:

- Change personalities mid‑testimony
- Reveal new evidence spontaneously
- Engage in slapstick physicality
- Treat perjury as a hobby

Their behavior mirrors dada performance art, where the goal is to destabilize meaning and expose the artificiality of social roles.

3.3 Evidence as Magical Realism

Evidence appears with:

- Dramatic sound effects
- Visual exaggeration
- Impossible timing

This transforms evidence from a legal object into a narrative artifact, emphasizing that in both games and real trials, evidence is always mediated.

---

4. The BuzzFeed Lawyer as Cultural Intermediary

The BuzzFeed video functions as a meta‑text: a real lawyer evaluating a fictional courtroom. Her commentary reveals several key insights.

4.1 The Lawyer’s Shock as Pedagogical Moment

Aizman repeatedly expresses disbelief at:

- The lack of rules of evidence
- The judge’s permissiveness
- The adversarial chaos
- The emotional manipulation

Her reactions highlight the spectacle of the Ace Attorney courtroom.

4.2 The Lawyer as Performer

Ironically, Aizman herself becomes a performer. Her role in the video mirrors Phoenix Wright’s role in the game:

- She interprets evidence (gameplay footage)
- She constructs narrative meaning
- She performs expertise for an audience

This parallel reinforces the idea that law is always performative.

4.3 The Lawyer’s Laughter as Critical Response

Her laughter is not merely amusement—it is a recognition of the absurdity inherent in legal systems. The game exaggerates what lawyers already know: trials are messy, unpredictable, and deeply human.

---

5. Procedural Rhetoric: What Ace Attorney Teaches About Law

5.1 Trials as Battles

The game frames trials as:

- Zero‑sum
- Emotionally charged
- Strategically adversarial

This reflects real‑world critiques of adversarial justice systems.

5.2 Truth as Contradiction

The core mechanic—finding contradictions—suggests that truth is:

- Fragmented
- Hidden
- Constructed through conflict

This aligns with post‑structuralist theories of discourse.

5.3 Justice as Narrative Resolution

In Ace Attorney, justice is achieved when:

- The narrative aligns
- The player feels closure
- The villain confesses dramatically

This reveals the narrative desires embedded in real legal systems.

6. Ace Attorney as Legal Satire

6.1 Exaggeration as Critique

The game exaggerates:

- Prosecutorial power
- Judicial incompetence
- Witness unreliability
- Defense desperation

These exaggerations expose real systemic anxieties.

6.2 The Tyranny of the Perfect Confession

Every case ends with a confession. This mirrors:

- Police interrogation culture
- Media obsession with closure
- The myth of the “smoking gun”

The BuzzFeed lawyer notes that real cases rarely end this neatly.

6.3 The Courtroom as Spectacle

Ace Attorney’s courtroom is closer to:

- Professional wrestling
- Reality TV
- Vaudeville theater

than to real legal practice. This is intentional: it critiques the spectacle of justice in modern media.

7. The Player as Jurist: Interactive Justice

7.1 The Player’s Role

The player is:

- Investigator
- Advocate
- Judge
- Jury

This collapses legal roles, revealing their constructed nature.

7.2 Emotional Truth vs. Legal Truth

The game rewards:

- Empathy
- Intuition
- Narrative coherence

rather than strict legal reasoning. This reflects real tensions between law and morality.

7.3 The Ethics of Play

Players often:

- Exploit witnesses
- Manipulate testimony
- Pressure confessions

The game normalizes ethically ambiguous tactics, raising questions about the moral pedagogy of legal games.

The game uses absurdity to:

- Expose legal anxieties
- Critique institutional authority

Ace Attorney’s cases resemble dada collages:

- Fragmented testimonies
- Disjointed evidence

9.3 The Courtroom as Anti‑Institution

The game’s courtroom is not a place of order—it is a space where order collapses, revealing the instability of legal authority.

10. Conclusion: Why Ace Attorney Matters

Ace Attorney is not a simulation of law. It is a critique, a performance, and a dadaist reimagining of the courtroom. The BuzzFeed lawyer’s reactions underscore the game’s power: by exaggerating legal absurdities, it reveals truths about the real justice system.

The game teaches players that:

- Truth is constructed
- Law is performative
- Justice is narrative
- Authority is fragile

In doing so, it transforms the courtroom into a site of playful yet profound inquiry.

# "Hold It!": The Avant-Garde Courtroom and Dadaist Legality in *Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney*
## Abstract
This paper analyzes Capcom’s *Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney* franchise as an exercise in Dadaist institutional subversion. Utilizing the 2019 *BuzzFeed Multiplayer* text featuring criminal defense attorney Diana Aizman as a legal realist baseline, we argue that the game’s procedural infrastructure operates not as an inaccurate legal simulation, but as an intentional *anti-realist* critique. By replacing established jurisprudential mechanics with avant-garde theatricality, narrative collage, and ludic absurdity, *Ace Attorney* destabilizes the Enlightenment myth of objective judicial truth.
## 1. Introduction: Law as Spectral Theater
When practicing criminal defense attorney Diana Aizman reacted to *Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney* for *BuzzFeed Multiplayer* in 2019, her reactions mapped a predictable baseline of professional incredulity. The lack of foundations for admitting evidence, the rampant perjury tolerated by the bench, and the immediate, unverified introduction of surprise witnesses all violate the core tenets of modern adversarial systems.
However, evaluating *Ace Attorney* through the lens of mere technical inaccuracy misses its primary cultural vector. The franchise does not suffer from poor legal research; it actively engages in a radical, interactive restructuring of the legal apparatus. The game moves beyond the "law-and-order" media tradition of comforting systemic competency into the realm of the anti-rational. It functions as a digital instantiation of Dadaism—an early 20th-century avant-garde movement that weaponized nonsense, chaos, and shock to dismantle bourgeois institutional authority.
## 2. Theoretical Anchors: Dadaism and Judicial Realism
### 2.1 Dadaist Shock Tactics (*Distancing the Bench*)
Dadaism sought to counter the hyper-rationalized, bureaucratic systems that ultimately led to the catastrophe of total warfare. Figures like Hugo Ball and Tristan Tzara utilized nonsense verse, spontaneous performances, and visual collages to mock the self-serious institutions of the state.
*Ace Attorney* translates these aesthetic disruptions into the digital legal arena:
 * **The Breakdown of Semiotics:** The iconic shouts of *"Objection!"*, *"Hold It!"*, and *"Take That!"* lose their purely functional legal definitions. They become acoustic shocks—linguistic weapons that visually rupture the screen, stunning the opposition into physical compliance regardless of the argument's substantive merit.
 * **Physical Transformation as Testimony:** Witnesses routinely shed their human identities on the stand. They devolve from mild-mannered citizens into literal mechanical puppets, whip-wielding dominatrices, or explosive cartoon caricatures under cross-examination. This mirrors Dadaist costume performances at the Cabaret Voltaire, which sought to strip human figures of civilized dignity to expose the underlying machinery of societal madness.

### 2.2 Critical Legal Realism and Ludic Epistemology
By contrasting the game's mechanics with Aizman's commentary, we can view the franchise through **Critical Legal Studies (CLS)**. Legal realism posits that judicial outcomes are never the product of pure, syllogistic deductions from neutral statutes; instead, they are driven by the biases, political realities, and psychological profiles of human actors.
*Ace Attorney* operationalizes this realism by making the judge entirely malleable. The bench is not an impartial computer processing logic, but a highly suggestible, easily confused individual swayed by emotional resonance and narrative flair. Truth within this *ludic epistemology* is not discovered via pristine forensic chains of custody. It is violently assembled by the player through the forced juxtaposition of contradictory data.
## 3. The Dadaist Courtroom: Anatomy of Absurd Justice
### 3.1 Structural Subversion of Procedure
In a standard courtroom, the rules of evidence act as a strict filter to protect the integrity of the record. In the *Ace Attorney* universe, these boundaries collapse into a structural collage. Evidence is introduced fluidly, mid-argument, without prior discovery or authenticating affidavits.
### 3.2 Comparative Structural Typology
| Dimension of Justice | The Enlightenment Courtroom (Real World) | The Dadaist Courtroom (*Ace Attorney*) |
|---|---|---|
| **Rules of Evidence** | Strict filters; exclusionary rules; chain of custody protocols. | Fluid, immediate, and narrative-driven; objects function as rhetorical talismans. |
| **Judicial Persona** | Emotionally detached, passive arbiter of statutory boundaries. | Highly reactive, impressionable, amnesiac, and governed by performance. |
| **Witness Ontology** | Bound by oath; legally restricted to personal, non-perjurious knowledge. | Metamorphic, unstable performers; perjury is an expected narrative state. |
| **Epistemological Aim** | Establishing structural probability beyond a reasonable doubt. | Forcing a theatrical confession via psychological and existential collapse. |
## 4. The Meta-Text: The Attorney as Spectator and Performer
The *BuzzFeed Multiplayer* video acts as a critical meta-textual bridge. As Aizman laughs at the game’s absurdity, her laughter serves an ideological purpose: it attempts to defend the dignity of real-world jurisprudence from the game's radical satire.
Yet, an ironic convergence occurs. To critique the game effectively for an online audience, Aizman must condense her professional expertise into bite-sized, engaging clips. She, too, must participate in a media spectacle. This dynamic underscores a deeper truth: both the real defense attorney and the fictional Phoenix Wright are bound by the demands of **performance**. A trial is never just about facts; it is an competitive exercise in storytelling where the most compelling narrative wins.
## 5. Conclusion: The Radical Utility of Absurdity
*Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney* does not offer a comforting simulation of a fair trial. Instead, it presents an explicitly anti-realist environment. By stripping the legal system of its sterile, procedural veneer, the game lays bare the underlying reality of the adversarial system: it is a high-stakes, deeply human theater where "truth" is manufactured through conflict.
By demanding that players navigate a world governed by Dadaist logic, *Ace Attorney* encourages a healthy skepticism toward institutional infallibility. It reminds us that justice is not a passive byproduct of bureaucracy—it is a messy, fragile, and deeply performative human construct.

Abstract

This paper argues that Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney should be understood not merely as an unrealistic representation of legal procedure but as a procedural critique of institutional truth-production. Drawing upon theories of Dadaism, legal realism, performance studies, and game studies, the paper examines how the franchise transforms the courtroom into a site of theatrical epistemology in which truth emerges through contradiction, spectacle, and player intervention rather than through formal procedure. Using criminal defense attorney Diana Aizman's 2019 commentary on the game's legal accuracy as a point of comparison, the analysis demonstrates that the distance between real jurisprudence and the game's legal universe is not a flaw of representation but a constitutive feature of its design. The game's persistent procedural absurdities function as a critique of legal authority itself, exposing the performative foundations underlying claims of objectivity and institutional neutrality.

Introduction

Among video games concerned with law, Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney occupies an unusual position. It is simultaneously one of the most recognizable legal games ever produced and one of the least realistic. Witnesses routinely lie with impunity. New evidence appears moments before verdicts. Prosecutors engage in overt misconduct. Judges are easily manipulated. Trials conclude within days and often culminate in dramatic confessions rather than evidentiary deliberation.

From the perspective of legal practice, these features appear absurd. Yet the game's enduring popularity suggests that realism is not its primary objective. The central question, therefore, is not why Ace Attorney fails to reproduce actual legal procedure but why it abandons procedural realism so thoroughly.

This paper proposes that the franchise's apparent irrationality should be understood as a deliberate aesthetic and epistemological strategy. Rather than functioning as a simulation of law, Ace Attorney functions as an interactive critique of institutional truth-production. The courtroom is transformed from a bureaucratic space into a theatrical arena in which authority, evidence, and narrative are constantly destabilized.

To explain this transformation, I draw on three interconnected traditions: Dadaist anti-institutional aesthetics, legal realist critiques of judicial objectivity, and contemporary game studies accounts of procedural rhetoric. Through this framework, the game emerges not as a failed representation of law but as an avant-garde reimagining of the courtroom.

Dadaism and Institutional Absurdity

Dada emerged during and after the First World War as a response to the perceived failure of rational European institutions. Artists associated with the movement rejected assumptions that bureaucracy, expertise, and reason necessarily produced social progress. Instead, they employed nonsense, fragmentation, irony, and performance to expose contradictions within modern systems.

Importantly, Dada was not merely chaotic. Its absurdity was strategic. Meaningless gestures, irrational performances, and deliberate disruptions were directed against institutions that presented themselves as coherent and authoritative.

The courtroom of Ace Attorney exhibits a strikingly similar structure.

On the surface, the game appears committed to the authority of legal institutions. Judges preside over proceedings. Attorneys make arguments. Witnesses testify. Evidence is introduced. Yet every one of these components is systematically destabilized.

The judge routinely misunderstands testimony.

Expert witnesses prove unreliable.

Physical evidence changes meaning repeatedly.

Legal procedures are abandoned whenever narrative tension requires it.

Authority exists, but authority never appears stable.

This is not simply parody. Rather, the game performs a sustained demonstration of institutional fragility. Like Dadaist performance, it stages the collapse of systems that claim rational legitimacy.

The Courtroom as Performance Space

Performance studies provides a second lens through which to understand the game.

Scholars such as Erving Goffman have argued that social institutions depend upon theatrical performances of legitimacy. Judges wear robes. Attorneys employ ritualized language. Courtrooms are organized around symbolic hierarchies. Authority is not merely exercised; it is performed.

Legal institutions often obscure this theatrical dimension by presenting procedure as neutral and objective. Ace Attorney does the opposite. It exaggerates theatricality until it becomes impossible to ignore.

Every major mechanic in the game is performative.

The famous cry of "Objection!" functions simultaneously as argument, spectacle, and interruption.

Cross-examination is presented less as fact-finding than as dramatic confrontation.

Witness breakdowns transform testimony into physical theater.

Court proceedings are governed by emotional reactions as much as legal reasoning.

By amplifying these elements, the game reveals dimensions of legal performance that ordinarily remain hidden.

The result is not a courtroom that lacks legitimacy but a courtroom in which legitimacy is visibly constructed in real time.

Legal Realism and the Instability of Truth

The game's treatment of truth aligns closely with themes developed by legal realism.

Classical legal theory often imagines judicial outcomes as the result of objective application of rules to facts. Legal realists challenged this assumption, arguing that legal decisions are shaped by interpretation, social context, institutional pressures, and human psychology.

Ace Attorney radicalizes this insight.

The game's central mechanic is contradiction.

Players do not discover truth directly. Instead, they identify inconsistencies between competing narratives.

Knowledge emerges negatively.

One learns what happened by demonstrating what could not have happened.

This process resembles neither scientific verification nor bureaucratic procedure. Rather, it resembles an adversarial struggle between narratives.

Truth appears not as an objective entity waiting to be found but as the provisional outcome of conflict.

The game's courtroom therefore serves as a dramatization of legal realist concerns. What matters is not simply evidence itself but who can successfully interpret, connect, and deploy evidence within a persuasive narrative framework.

Procedural Rhetoric and Interactive Epistemology

Game studies offers an additional conceptual tool through Ian Bogost's notion of procedural rhetoric. Games make arguments through rules. Their mechanics express claims about how the world works.

The procedural rhetoric of Ace Attorney can be summarized as follows:

Truth is hidden.

Institutions are unreliable.

Authority figures are fallible.

Contradictions reveal reality.

Persistence produces justice.

These claims are not communicated primarily through dialogue. They are embedded within gameplay systems.

Every case requires players to distrust official accounts.

Every investigation demonstrates institutional failure.

Every successful cross-examination rewards skepticism toward authority.

The player learns a philosophy of knowledge through action.

This distinguishes Ace Attorney from conventional courtroom dramas. Television legal dramas often persuade audiences through narrative resolution. Ace Attorney persuades through procedural participation. The player personally experiences the instability of legal truth.

Diana Aizman's Commentary as External Validation

The 2019 BuzzFeed Multiplayer video featuring criminal defense attorney Diana Aizman is particularly valuable because it provides a real-world legal perspective on the game's mechanics.

Her commentary repeatedly identifies violations of actual legal procedure. She notes problems involving evidentiary foundations, witness management, judicial conduct, and prosecutorial ethics.

At first glance, these observations appear to undermine the game's credibility.

However, they may instead reveal its central strategy.

Aizman's reactions establish the precise distance between legal reality and game design. That distance is not accidental. It is the space in which the game's critique operates.

The stronger the contrast between actual procedure and game procedure, the more visible the assumptions underlying legal institutions become.

The lawyer's disbelief therefore serves as evidence not of design failure but of successful estrangement.

The game makes law appear strange.

By making law strange, it becomes available for critique.

Counterargument: Is Ace Attorney Merely Comedy?

One might object that this interpretation overstates the game's intellectual ambitions.

Perhaps Ace Attorney is simply a comedic adventure game whose absurdities exist solely for entertainment value.

This objection cannot be dismissed entirely. The franchise is undeniably humorous and often prioritizes narrative excitement over thematic consistency.

Yet comedy and critique are not mutually exclusive.

Historically, satire has frequently employed exaggeration to expose institutional contradictions. The question is not whether the game is funny but what its humor accomplishes.

The recurring targets of the game's comedy are revealing. Judges, prosecutors, police investigators, expert witnesses, forensic systems, and legal procedures are all subjected to systematic destabilization.

The pattern is too consistent to be reduced to random comic invention.

The game repeatedly directs attention toward failures of institutional authority.

Its comedy therefore performs critical work.

Conclusion

Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney should not be evaluated primarily according to standards of legal accuracy. Such an approach misunderstands the nature of the project.

The franchise functions as an interactive exploration of how institutions construct truth. Drawing upon strategies that parallel Dadaist aesthetics, it transforms the courtroom into a site of productive absurdity. Authority becomes unstable. Procedure becomes theatrical. Truth emerges through contradiction rather than administrative process.

The result is a legal universe that appears irrational yet expresses a coherent epistemological argument.

The game's courtroom is not a failed version of law.

It is a deliberately estranged version of law.

By making legal institutions absurd, Ace Attorney reveals how much of their authority depends upon performance, narrative, and belief. Its lasting significance lies not in teaching players how courts work but in encouraging them to question how truth, legitimacy, and justice are produced in the first place.
