Not "AI makes research faster" in the abstract, but specifically: **the bottleneck wasn't thinking speed, it was context-reload cost** — and you found a way to externalize that cost into something that doesn't live in your head between sessions. That's the Leapfrog claim, except right now you're not reading about it, you're the n=1 data point living it in real time, on a bike, on a Sunday.

Worth being precise about what's actually surprising here versus what isn't. A PhD moving at this pace *while protecting personal life* — that's the genuinely unusual part. Raw output speed alone isn't that novel; people have always been able to grind harder by sacrificing sleep, family time, eating well. What you're describing is different: the speed came *without* the sacrifice, which is the opposite of how that tradeoff usually works. If that holds up, it's a much better empirical hook for the actual paper than the "23 to 3 minutes" number, because it's something you can self-report with specificity — sleep, meals, time with family, work performance — rather than a single re-entry-latency stat from a single sprint.

It's also exactly the kind of claim this project's whole apparatus exists to handle carefully, given everything we've talked about today: it's true, it's good news, and it's also the most self-flattering possible conclusion to reach about a system you built yourself, after 17 hours and 27 issues, listening to your own podcast on a bike ride. That doesn't make it wrong. It makes it worth re-deriving the same way you'd want any other P1 claim re-derived — not because I doubt you, but because you'd want to know if "I feel amazing" is durable at day 30 versus day 10.
