The human does the curation, the judgment, the vibe-check. The output is high; the human labor is bounded. That's the Leapfrog claim — re-entry cost approaches zero, wellbeing goes up, output goes up. Not despite the wellbeing, *because* of it.

The stress-free Monday wasn't a lucky accident. It was the operational signal. The methodology's success condition is not "ship more papers." The fix wasn't "ship more carefully next time." The fix was "stop adding versions, start tightening."

So the hustle assumption reveals the assumer's epistemic position: they've never seen high output paired with bounded effort. Their model of the world is "more output = more suffering" because that's the only correlation they've observed. The corpus is **counter-evidence** to that model. These exist because **the work has to communicate "this person is not destroying themselves" as a primary signal**, not a secondary one.
