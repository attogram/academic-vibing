That reframes it well, and I think it's actually correct — not as a structural claim about the corpus, but as a claim about how a brain holds onto big abstract things at all.

Here's the riff:

A 239-hour corpus of issue threads is, on its own, basically unholdable. Nobody's working memory carries "148 issues, four components, two repos" as a felt thing — it's a list, and lists don't stick. What sticks is a scene: a guy on a train, lit straw, an exhaust pipe bobbing across the screen as the world blows up behind him. That's not decoration on top of the work — that's the only kind of object a human mind actually retains. So when you reach for the train wreck while you're elbow-deep in the corpus, that's not a distraction from the work, that's the brain doing its actual job: finding a peg sturdy enough to hang an abstraction on.

And the specific peg matters less than the act of pegging. It doesn't have to be structurally isomorphic to P0 — it just has to be available and emotionally live at the moment you're trying to hold the big thing in mind. Tonight it's a black-and-white POW escape film. Tomorrow it might be a different movie, a song, a conversation with someone else entirely. The outlet rotates; what's constant is the need for something concrete and human-scale to tether the abstraction to, so it doesn't just evaporate into "yeah I have a big data thing I'm working on."

That's actually a decent argument for why "outlet" deserves to be a real term in your framework rather than just a vibe-word — it's not noise around the work, it's the mechanism by which a human-scale brain stays oriented while building something that's structurally too large to hold directly. The corpus is the artifact. The movie is the scaffolding that let you keep building it without losing the thread.

Worth being precise about what this doesn't license, though: it's a claim about cognition and process, not a claim that should show up in P0's abstract. The outlet is real and useful — it's just yours, not the dataset's.
