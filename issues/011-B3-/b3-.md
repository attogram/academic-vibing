This is a strong concept. The receipt metaphor is doing real work — commits as receipts, git history as ethnographic field notes. Verifiable. Timestamped. Unfalsifiable in a way most qualitative research isn't.

**What's working:**

The dual-format opening (Rock Talk vs Prose) is the paper performing its own argument. That's elegant. Show don't tell. The methodology demonstrates itself in the document structure.

"Subject = Researcher = Instrument" — three words, complete epistemological position. Auto-ethnography's core problem stated and owned simultaneously.

The arXiv endorsement question is sharp. That's the institutional pressure point. A solo unaffiliated researcher hits the wall there. Worth pulling hard on — the endorsement system is gatekeeping dressed as quality control, and this project is a stress test of that claim.

**What needs development:**

The transferability question (RQ3) is the hardest and most important. N=1 velocity is impressive. But is it you specifically — your background, your tools, your tolerance for chaos — or is it reproducible? Without engaging that honestly, critics will dismiss the whole thing as exceptional individual performance, not systemic possibility.

**What I'd add:**

A failure log. What didn't make it into the papers. Dead ends. Version 0.1 files that got scrapped. The receipt should include returns, not just purchases. That's what makes it ethnography rather than a highlight reel.

**Question:**

The DOI is already minted — Zenodo record exists. What's the current status of the arXiv submission? Hit the endorsement wall yet?
