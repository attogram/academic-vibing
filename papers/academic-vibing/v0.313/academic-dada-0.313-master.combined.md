### [PROSE]
This paper introduces the **Consensus Divergence Index (CDI)**, a mathematical metric for evaluating the reliability of research claims generated by multi-agent systems (MAS). By measuring the semantic friction between disparate Large Language Model (LLM) architectures, the CDI identifies the "Research Boundary"—points where existing model biases are insufficient to form a consensus, thereby signaling where human verification is most critical. We demonstrate the utility of this metric through a longitudinal n=1 study on agentic research velocity and context re-entry costs.

## 1. Introduction

As Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly integrated into research workflows, the risk of "Synthetic Consensus"—where multiple models agree on a false or biased claim due to shared training data—becomes a primary bottleneck for research integrity. Traditional human-centric verification methods cannot keep pace with the velocity of AI-assisted synthesis. Academic Vibing is a meta-methodology designed to solve this velocity-integrity gap by utilizing architectural diversity as an adversarial audit layer [1].

---

## 2. Methodology: The Consensus Divergence Index (CDI)

The CDI formalizes the intuition that disagreement between models is a higher-trust signal than agreement. When model architectures from different families (e.g., Claude, Gemini, GPT) evaluation a claim and diverge, they highlight the epistemic limits of the current substrate.

### 2.1 Formal Definition
We define the CDI as the ratio of disputed claims ($d$) to the total number of claims evaluating ($n$):

$$CDI = \frac{d}{n}$$

Where a claim is considered disputed if at least one model in the swarm provides a logic-grounded dissent to the majority conclusion.

### 2.2 Thresholds and Interpretation
- **High CDI ($\ge 0.5$):** The Research Boundary. High signal; indicates that the claim requires deep human re-derivation.
- **Low CDI ($\le 0.1$):** The Consensus Trap. Low signal; indicates that models are likely mirroring shared training data or sycophantic patterns rather than independently validating the claim.

## 3. Context Persistence and the Leapfrog Mechanism

A secondary discovery of the CDI-audited workflow is the reduction of task-switching costs. We hypothesize the existence of the **Leapfrog Mechanism**, wherein the externalization of research state into a structured, persistent "Issue-Loop" reduces context re-entry time from the industry standard of 23 minutes [10] to less than 3 minutes.

### 3.1 Exocognitive State Stores
By treating a version-controlled repository as a functional extension of working memory (The Extended Mind [5]), the researcher decouples "dropping context" from "grabbing context." The GitHub Issue-Loop serves as a persistent state store ([STATE], [BLOCKER], [PAYLOAD]), ensuring that the MAS can maintain long-term research continuity across disconnected sessions.

---

## 4. Hardware Independence and Efficiency

A core claim of this methodology is that the protocol is more critical than raw compute power. The entirety of the Attogram Corpus, including the CDI-audited v0.10 synthesis, was generated using consumer-grade hardware (16GB RAM) and low-latency APIs. This proves that structured curiosity and adversarial auditing can yield high-integrity research outcomes without the need for frontier-scale infrastructure.

---

## 5. Conclusion

The transition from exploratory "vibing" to the formalized Consensus Divergence Index provides a robust framework for high-velocity research. By measuring friction instead of simple overlap, the CDI offers a quantifiable defense against synthetic bias and establishes a new standard for human-agent collaboration in technical domains.
