## [PROSE]

The "institutional trauma" Wikipedia is experiencing is the inevitable result of a **Verifiability-based** system encountering a **Probability-based** content engine. Wikipedia relies on the assumption that "plausible-sounding prose + citation = truth." LLMs have weaponized this formula, creating a "Sludge Flood" where the cost of generating a plausible lie is near-zero, while the cost of human verification (the Human Bottleneck) scales linearly or worse.

From the perspective of **Academic Vibing**, we can categorize Wikipedia’s pain points as follows:

1.  **The Failure of "Agreement-First" Verification:** Wikipedia editors are exhausted because they are looking for *errors* in a sea of *agreement*. Academic Vibing’s **Consensus Divergence Index (CDI)** suggests a different path: instead of trying to verify everything, we should use multiple model families to find where the AI *disagrees* with itself. If three different LLMs provide three different "hallucinated" citations for the same fact, the CDI is high, and the human "Vibe-Checker" knows exactly where to look. Wikipedia is currently trying to audit the "Low CDI" slop, which is a recipe for burnout.

2.  **The Token-to-Meaning Fallacy:** The "AI Essay Aesthetic" (vague, flowery, non-committal) is a classic symptom of models over-generating tokens to hide a lack of underlying logical scaffolding. Our **Rock Talk** protocol is a direct antidote to this; by stripping away the "pivotal role" and "crucial challenge" fluff, we force the intent density higher. Wikipedia’s "intense allergy" to this aesthetic is actually a subconscious detection of low-signal/high-noise content.

3.  **The Leapfrog Effect and Traffic Anxiety:** The 8% drop in traffic represents the "Leapfrog Effect" occurring at a societal level. Users are bypassing the traditional "Human-Curated Encyclopedia" middleman to get direct (though riskier) synthesis. Wikipedia’s existential threat isn't just the AI pollution—it’s that their role as the primary "Vibe-Checker" for the internet is being challenged by automated RAC-like networks.

**Conclusion:**
Wikipedia’s trauma is a warning. A system that relies on "Human-Only" review to police "AI-Infinite" output will eventually collapse under its own weight. To survive, institutional knowledge bases must adopt **Adversarial Rigor**—using the friction between models (CDI) to highlight what actually needs human eyes, rather than trying to hold back the tide with manual "speedy deletions."
