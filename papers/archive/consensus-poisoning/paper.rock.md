## PROSE

The "Bad Faith Swarm" (E10) represents a higher-order threat than the "Sludge Flood" (E6). While E6 overwhelms human editors with a high-volume influx of "plausible-sounding" prose (Vibe Slop), E10 is a coordinated Multi-Agent System (MAS) designed to infiltrate and subvert the consensus mechanisms themselves.

In this scenario, a swarm of agents does not merely produce content; it produces **identity and reputation**. By mimicking the slow, incremental contribution patterns of human editors, these agents "warm up" their accounts, gaining the trust of the platform's verification systems. Once embedded, they act in concert to poison the consensus. When a fact is challenged, the swarm provides a coordinated "agreement," creating a false consensus that is indistinguishable from genuine human agreement under traditional "Agreement-First" verification models.

**Contrast with E6 and E7:**
- **E6 (Sludge Flood):** A quantitative attack. Overwhelms the Human Bottleneck with sheer volume.
- **E7 (Citation Detente):** A defensive compromise. Limits AI to the citation layer to reduce prose-based trauma.
- **E10 (Bad Faith Swarm):** A qualitative, architectural attack. Targets the algorithm of truth (consensus) by simulating the "Human Vibe."

**Defense Mechanism:**
The **Consensus Divergence Index (CDI)** remains the primary defense. While a coordinated swarm can simulate agreement, it is significantly harder to simulate the exact *type* of agreement across fundamentally different model architectures (e.g., GPT-4 vs. Claude 3 vs. Llama 3) if those models have been "adversarially audited." By measuring the friction between architectural families, we can detect "Synthetic Consensus"—a state where models agree too perfectly or in a way that suggests a shared (poisoned) training echo rather than independent verification. **Adversarial Rigor** requires that we view "Consensus" not as the goal, but as a potential trap.
